# Theory of Dilute Solutions: Colligative Properties

Date: December 14, 2025

## Introduction
Theory of dilute solutions focuses on properties depending on solute particle count, not type. From notes: Colligative means "held together"; properties include vapor pressure lowering, boiling point elevation, freezing point depression, osmotic pressure.

## Key Concepts in Ordered List
1. **Definition of Dilute Solution**: Solute concentration low; properties approximate ideal behavior.
2. **Colligative Properties**: Depend on particle number. For electrolytes, include van't Hoff factor (i) for dissociation (e.g., NaCl i=2).
3. **Vapor Pressure Lowering (Raoult's Law)**: Relative lowering = (P0 - P)/P0 = x_solute (mole fraction). Notes: Non-volatile solute reduces vapor pressure.
4. **Boiling Point Elevation**: ΔTb = i Kb m (molality). Notes: Increase in particles causes elevation; examples NaCl, KCl dissociation.
5. **Freezing Point Depression**: ΔTf = i Kf m. Applications: Road salt, antifreeze.
6. **Osmotic Pressure**: Π = i c R T (c concentration). Notes: Semipermeable membrane effect.
7. **Abnormal Colligative Properties**: For electrolytes, deviation due to dissociation; van't Hoff factor corrects.

## Table: Colligative Properties Summary

| Property                  | Formula                  | Dependence                  | Textile Application         |
|---------------------------|--------------------------|-----------------------------|-----------------------------|
| Vapor Pressure Lowering   | (P0 - P)/P0 = x_solute   | Mole fraction               | Dye solution stability      |
| Boiling Point Elevation   | ΔTb = Kb m               | Molality                    | Boiling in wet processing   |
| Freezing Point Depression | ΔTf = Kf m               | Molality                    | Freeze-drying fabrics       |
| Osmotic Pressure          | Π = c R T                | Concentration               | Membrane filtration         |

## Enhanced Information
Thermodynamic basis: Derived from chemical potential changes. In 2025, simulations using AI model non-ideal behaviors. Notes from multiple PDFs emphasize Raoult's law and examples; enhanced with real-world: In textiles, colligative effects influence dye baths.

# Chemical Equilibrium

Date: December 14, 2025

## Introduction
Chemical equilibrium is a dynamic state where forward and reverse rates are equal. From notes: Concentrations constant; equilibrium constant is ratio of product to reactant active masses.

## Key Concepts in Ordered List
1. **Definition**: Forward and reverse reactions at same rate. Notes: A + B ⇌ C + D; rates equal at equilibrium.
2. **Law of Active Mass**: Rate ∝ active masses (concentration or partial pressure). Notes: Active mass of pure solids/liquids = 1.
3. **Equilibrium Constant (K)**: K = [products]/[reactants] for concentrations (Kc); or partial pressures (Kp). Notes: Ratio of active masses.
4. **Le Chatelier's Principle**: System shifts to counter changes (temperature, pressure, concentration). Enhanced: Temperature affects endothermic/exothermic reactions.
5. **Factors Affecting Equilibrium**: Temperature (van't Hoff equation), pressure (for gases), concentration.
6. **Applications**: In textiles, equilibrium in acid-base dyeing processes.
7. **Calculations**: Using ICE tables for initial, change, equilibrium concentrations.

## Table: Types of Equilibrium Constants

| Type | Expression           | Application              |
|------|-----------------------|--------------------------|
| Kc   | [C]^c [D]^d / [A]^a [B]^b | Homogeneous solutions    |
| Kp   | (Pc)^c (Pd)^d / (Pa)^a (Pb)^b | Gas phase reactions      |
| Ksp  | Product ions concentrations | Solubility equilibria    |

## Enhanced Information
Dynamic nature: Molecules continue reacting, but net change zero. Recent 2025 studies use quantum computing for complex equilibria. Notes detail active mass calculations, e.g., mole fraction for partial pressure.

# Chemical Kinetics

Date: December 14, 2025

## Introduction
Chemical kinetics studies reaction rates and mechanisms. From notes: Branch of physical chemistry dealing with rates, factors, mechanisms.

## Key Concepts in Ordered List
1. **Rate of Reaction**: Change in concentration per time. Notes: -d[A]/dt for reactants, +d[B]/dt for products.
2. **Rate Law**: Rate = k [reactants]^order. Order: zero, first, second, etc.
3. **Factors Affecting Rate**: Concentration, temperature (Arrhenius: k = A e^{-Ea/RT}), pressure, catalysts, light.
4. **Molecularity**: Number of molecules in elementary step; unimolecular, bimolecular.
5. **Mechanism**: Sequence of elementary steps. Notes: Objective includes mechanism.
6. **Integrated Rate Laws**: For first-order: ln[A] = ln[A0] - kt.
7. **Applications in Textiles**: Enzyme kinetics in bio-processing.

## Table: Order of Reactions

| Order | Rate Law     | Integrated Law          | Half-Life             |
|-------|--------------|-------------------------|-----------------------|
| Zero  | k            | [A] = [A0] - kt        | [A0]/2k               |
| First | k[A]         | ln[A] = ln[A0] - kt     | 0.693/k               |
| Second| k[A]^2       | 1/[A] = 1/[A0] + kt    | 1/(k[A0])             |

## Enhanced Information
Arrhenius equation quantifies temperature effect; Ea is activation energy. In 2025, machine learning predicts kinetics. Notes cover zero to nth order, factors like amphoterism.

# Light Absorption and Beer-Lambert Law

Date: December 14, 2025

## Introduction
Light absorption occurs when photons are taken up by matter, leading to photochemical reactions. Beer-Lambert law quantifies this. From notes: Intensity reduces exponentially; ln(I/I0) = -ε C x.

## Key Concepts in Ordered List
1. **Absorption Definition**: Part of light absorbed in medium; intensity I = photons per area per time.
2. **Derivation**: dI/I = -b dx; integrates to I = I0 e^{-bx}. For solutions: b = ε C (molar absorptivity times concentration).
3. **Beer-Lambert Law**: A = ε c l, where A = log10(I0/I) (absorbance).
4. **Assumptions**: Monochromatic light, dilute solutions, no scattering.
5. **Limitations**: Deviations at high concentrations due to interactions.
6. **Applications**: Spectrophotometry for concentration measurement in dyes.
7. **Photochemistry Link**: Absorbed light causes reactions; basis for analysis.

## Table: Variables in Beer-Lambert Law

| Symbol | Meaning                     | Unit                  |
|--------|-----------------------------|-----------------------|
| A      | Absorbance                  | Unitless              |
| ε      | Molar absorptivity          | L mol^{-1} cm^{-1}    |
| c      | Concentration               | mol L^{-1}            |
| l      | Path length                 | cm                    |

## Enhanced Information
The law is foundational for UV-Vis spectroscopy. Recent extensions include multi-wavelength analysis. Notes derive from photon fraction absorbed.
